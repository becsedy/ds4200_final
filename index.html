Group 26: Benjamin Ecsedy, Andrew Loose, Yash Nalluri


Diabetes and Its Importance

Diabetes refers to a set of diseases that results in high blood glucose, coming in two main types: type 1, where the pancreas cannot make enough insulin to regulate oneâ€™s blood sugar level, and type 2, where the pancreas does make insulin, but the body is unable to use this insulin optimally, also leading to a high level of blood glucose. Diabetes is a major global health concern, affecting millions of people of all ages, potentially increasing the risk for those with diabetes to have serious health complications such as heart disease, kidney failure, nerve damage, and vision loss. Those suffering from diabetes, especially in the United States, often bear a large financial burden to be able to afford their life-sustaining insulin. Several factors can increase the chances of oneâ€™s development of diabetes, including obesity, physical inactivity, an imbalanced diet, high blood pressure, and their familyâ€™s history of the disease. Age also plays a role, as the risk increases with aging, and certain ethnic groups are more predisposed to developing diabetes. Investigating the factors that lead to diabetes through visualization and data analysis is essential for prevention, early diagnosis, and effective management of the disease, as well as unlocking the ability to develop targeted public health strategies, promote lifestyle modifications, and create better treatment options to reduce the growing prevalence of diabetes to help millions of individuals worldwide.


Our Dataset

This investigation uses the "Diabetes 012 Health Indicators" dataset from the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System (BRFSS) 2015, collected by the CDC. It includes health-related risk behaviors, chronic conditions, and preventive service usage. The dataset contains 253,680 patient observations and 22 attributes, exceeding the 10,000-observation limit for this project. To comply, we will randomly sample 10,000 observations while preserving the distribution of the target variable (Diabetes 012). If needed, stratified sampling will ensure proportional representation of diabetes categories. This dataset provides a broad view of key diabetes-related health indicators, enabling analysis of correlations between lifestyle factors and diabetes risk. It includes both categorical (e.g., smoking status, general health) and continuous variables (e.g., BMI, mental health days), making it suitable for visualization and modeling.


The Diabetes (012) variable classifies individuals as follows:

  • 0: No diabetes
  • 1: Prediabetes or borderline diabetes
  • 2: Diagnosed diabetes

Feature Analysis

Because the development of diabetes is a complex, multifaceted medical issue, there are many warning signs that can come into play when trying to predict the onset of the disease. Some factors are well within a patient's control, and would serve as the basis for advice given to a patient from their doctor about controlling lifestyle and habits, while some factors are genetically predisposed and not up to the control of the patient. Some factors, however, are somewhere in between. For example, a patient's cholesterol level is somewhat dictated by their diet and exercise choices, yet also many people are genetically predisposed to high cholesterol. Thus, we found it useful in this analysis to divide the most important features into two sections that can be individually addressed by our visualizations and analysis: uncontrollable (or immutable, both are used interchangeably), which represent health/lifestyle predispositions that are fully or mostly out of control of the patient, and controllable (or mutable), which are factors that are fully or mostly in the patient's control. Dividing the factors into these categories not only makes each visualization more cohesive and contextual, but can allow both doctors and patients alike to focus on factors that are actively in the patient's control, while placing potential lifestyle changes within the context of the patient's past/family medical history.



But what factors are the most important to analyze in the first place? It is important to note that although it is possible to make lifestyle changes that can impact one's BMI in hopes of mitigating one's risk of diabetes, because there are so many genetic predispositions to one's BMI, and because of all of the interconnectedness that one's BMI has with other health and genetic features, it made the msot sense to group BMI with the other immutable features. Furthermore, physical health and mental health are separate from general health, as general health refers to the complex network of genetic disease, family history, lifestyle, and bodily composition. In contrast, physical and mental health are measured simply by identifying the number of days per month that one feels physically or mentally unwell, respectively, which is, for the most part, a mutable quantity. Thus, a high mental/physical health refers to fewer days per month feeling unwell, and a low mental/physical health refers to more days per month feeling unwell.
